River Network of Nepal

Nepal is often referred to as "The second richest country in water resources." While this claim might be an exaggeration, it is undeniable that Nepal possesses significant water resources. Many of its rivers originate from the snow-covered mountains, while others begin in the Mid-Hill region. Additionally, numerous smaller rivulets originate from the Chure region, a young lowland area of Nepal. Koshi, Gandaki and Karnali (from east to west) are the three major river systems of Nepal.
I downloaded the data from HydroSHEDS and developed the river network of Nepal.

Physiographic Zones of Nepal

Nepal boasts one of the most unique physiographies in the world. Within a span of about 200 km, the altitude changes dramatically from 60 meters above sea level to 8,848 meters above sea level. Nepal is classified into five distinct physiographic regions: High Himalaya, High Mountain, Hill, Siwalik, and Terai/Tarai. A journey from the southernmost plains of the Terai to the northern High Himalayas allows one to experience extremely hot temperatures, the cool breezes of the Mid-Hills, and the snowy regions of the High Himalayas—all in a single day.

Suitable Habitat of Bambusa nepalensis

Bamboo, often referred to as "green gold," holds immense value in Nepali livelihood and culture. In Nepali Hindu traditions, bamboo plays a crucial role from birth to death. It is used in various rites and rituals. In rural Nepal, bamboo is integral to daily life, being used to make baskets and other essential items. It is also an important fodder species for livestock.
Additionally, bamboo is frequently planted in areas prone to landslides and floods to help prevent such disasters due to its strong root system and soil-stabilizing properties.
I am currently leading a research project focused on the suitable habitats of seven bamboo species in Nepal through ecological niche modeling. One of the species we are studying is Bambusa nepalensis.

Bird Collision

In contemporary times, bird collisions with infrastructure have become a significant concern. Birds frequently collide with towers, tall buildings, poles, and high-tension power lines. These collisions often result in fatalities, with more than half of the incidents proving deadly for the birds. While such deaths might not substantially impact common bird species, they pose a serious threat to endangered or threatened bird populations.
