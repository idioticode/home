The ts() function converts the numeric sales data into a time series object.

A time series object in R organizes the data with respect to time, which is essential for analysis and modeling. Without this structure, many time series functions, such as plotting, decomposition, and modeling, would not work effectively.


4. Visualizing the Time Series Data

Visualization is the first step in time series analysis. A time series plot allows us to examine key characteristics of the data:

  • Trend: A long-term increase or decrease in the data.
  • Seasonality: Regular patterns or cycles within specific time intervals (e.g., months, quarters).
  • Irregularities/Anomalies: Random fluctuations or outliers in the data.
  • For this dataset, we aim to identify if there is an upward or downward trend or repetitive seasonal patterns.
plot(smp_ts)


5. Stationarity in Time Series

Stationarity is a fundamental assumption in many time series modeling methods, including ARIMA. A stationary time series has a constant mean, variance, and autocorrelation structure over time.

Non-stationary data can lead to unreliable models because they are driven by trends or external factors rather than intrinsic patterns.


6. Autocorrelation and Partial Autocorrelation Functions

Acf(smp_ts)

Pacf(smp_ts)

The Autocorrelation Function (ACF) measures the correlation between the time series and its lagged versions. High autocorrelations at certain lags indicate dependence. The Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF) isolates the correlation of a specific lag after accounting for all previous lags. This helps in identifying autoregressive (AR) terms in ARIMA models.

7. Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) Test for Stationarity

smp_ts |> adf.test()
## 
##  Augmented Dickey-Fuller Test
## 
## data:  smp_ts
## Dickey-Fuller = -0.93943, Lag order = 3, p-value = 0.9332
## alternative hypothesis: stationary
  • The ADF Test evaluates the null hypothesis : The time series is non-stationary.
  • A high p-value (p>0.05) means we fail to reject the null-hypothesis, confirming the series is non-stationary.
  • This is expected in real-world datasets with trends or seasonality.


Determining ARIMA Parameters (p,d,q)

The ARIMA model combines:

  • Autoregressive (AR) component (p): Captures the relationship between an observation and its lagged values.
  • Differencing (I) component (d): Makes the series stationary.
  • Moving Average (MA) component (q): Models the relationship between an observation and the residual errors from lagged observations.


8. Differencing to Achieve Stationarity

Differencing removes trends or cycles in the data, stabilizing its mean and variance over time. Here, we apply first-order differencing (d=1), which calculates the difference between consecutive observations.

smp_ts |> diff(differences = 1) |> adf.test()
## Warning in adf.test(diff(smp_ts, differences = 1)): p-value smaller than
## printed p-value
## 
##  Augmented Dickey-Fuller Test
## 
## data:  diff(smp_ts, differences = 1)
## Dickey-Fuller = -5.9245, Lag order = 3, p-value = 0.01
## alternative hypothesis: stationary

After differencing, the ADF test confirms stationarity (p-value < 0.05). This step is critical for making the data suitable for ARIMA modeling. First differencing has given us stationary data. therefore, the d part of our model is 1.

Plotting the model for a visual interpretation.

smp_ts |> diff(differences = 1) |> plot()


Now we determine p and q parameters with differencing 1.


9. Identifying AR (p) Using PACF

Pacf(smp_ts |> diff(differences = 1))

In the PACF plot, significant spikes outside the confidence bounds suggest potential AR terms. The lag with the highest significant spike is chosen as p.In this case, the first lag is significant (p=1).

10. Identifying MA (q) Using ACF

Acf(smp_ts |> diff(differences = 1))

In the ACF plot, significant spikes indicate potential MA terms. Here, the first and second lags are significant, so q=2 is chosen.

11. Building the ARIMA Model

ARIMA(1,1,2) specifies p=1, d=1, and q=2.

tsmod <- Arima(smp_ts, order = c(1, 1, 2))

Diagnostic Analysis of the ARIMA Model

11. Box-Ljung Test for Residuals

The Box-Ljung Test evaluates whether the residuals from the model are independent (white noise). If the p-value is high (p>0.05), the residuals are uncorrelated, indicating a well-fitted model.

Box.test(tsmod$residuals, type = "Ljung-Box")
## 
##  Box-Ljung test
## 
## data:  tsmod$residuals
## X-squared = 5.5599e-06, df = 1, p-value = 0.9981

The extremely high p-value (close to 1) suggests that the residuals are not autocorrelated. This is an excellent result, confirming that the ARIMA(1,1,2) model successfully captures all patterns in the data.

  • Alternatively, Box-Pierce test also can be performed.


12. ACF and PACF of Residuals

To confirm that the residuals are white noise, we examine their ACF and PACF plots.

Acf(tsmod$residuals)

Pacf(tsmod$residuals)

The ACF and PACF plots should show no significant spikes (all points within the confidence bounds). Lack of significant spikes confirms that the residuals have no remaining patterns, trends, or dependencies that the model failed to capture.


13. checkresiduals Function

Good residusals have no discernible pattern (white noise), and are uncorrelated (Ljung-Box test with high p-value).

checkresiduals(tsmod)

## 
##  Ljung-Box test

##

The p value (p=0.4231) is comfortably above 0.05, affirming that the residuals are uncorrelated. The test confirms that the ARIMA(1,1,2) model adequately captures the time series structure.


14. Forecasting Future Values

fcast <- forecast(tsmod, h = 6, bootstrap = TRUE, times = 100)
## Warning in forecast.forecast_ARIMA(tsmod, h = 6, bootstrap = TRUE, times =
## 100): The non-existent times arguments will be ignored.
  • The forecast function predicts future values.
  • Bootstrap forecasting generates multiple simulations to create robust confidence intervals.
  • The h = 6 specifies a 6-step forecast horizon. It means the forecast is done for next six times. We can specify the numbers based on our requirements.

The forecast includes: * Point Forecasts: Central predictions for future values. * Confidence Intervals (80%, 95%): Ranges where future values are likely to fall. Wider intervals indicate higher uncertainty.
